The Storyteller's Dictionary

You can't learn how to write novels. You can only learn how to write the novel you're currently writing.

Brandon Sanderson (paraphrased)

There's no such thing as a hard-and-fast rule when it comes to creative work, and unfortunately storytelling is no different. The criteria for what is considered a good story will vary between cultures all the way down to individual tastes. No unified theory could ever explain what is compelling and emotionally stirring about every> story in the world.

There are, however, a handful of patterns that stories share—in the Western world, at least. Writers and academics have a lot of fun bundling these patterns into what I call storytelling methods, some of which you may have been taught in school. Each method has internally consistent rules that are applicable within the confines of that method. If you're writing a long-form story for a Western audience, the Storyteller's Dictionary can help you.

No method is more "accurate" than another or cuts more deeply into the essence of storytelling. There are stories I love that contradict much of what I've written here, and a hypothetical story that follows all of this advice wouldn't necessarily be good. But one or more of these methods may be useful for the purpose at hand or shed light on a story you're struggling to tell.

Who is this site for?


The Storyteller's Dictionary is written using language that addresses writers of prose fiction, particularly novelists. This is purely for the sake of consistency and clarity—you can imagine how tiring it would be to read "novel/screenplay/short story/TV script/etc" several times on every page. You should be able to swap out the appropriate words to whichever medium you choose and the concepts will still apply.

There will be spoilers.
